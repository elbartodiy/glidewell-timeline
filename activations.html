Glidewell documentary · beyond the film

Two outputs, not one.

The documentary is the film. But the same material — footage, archives, artifacts — can also become an interactive living history: an audience-facing experience people walk into, and a legacy they can revisit years from now to find an interview or trace how the craft changed.

A working proposal. Real event names, internal touchpoints and budget are marked to fill.

How we pitch each activation

Every activation is described the same way we describe a film block — so it reads in one consistent shape:

  • TaskWhich people-flow or moment is this for?
  • What we needSpace, hardware, content, staffing.
  • WhyThe value to land — the message, the thing we want them to feel or do (learn / be sold / feel part of the community).
  • MechanicHow it works — the actual interaction.
  • OutcomeWhat the visitor walks away with: now understands, knows, and likes Glidewell.

Three seed use-cases

01 · Symposium

An interactive board, as a mini-quest

Three stations, optionally linked into one route (“solve this here → go there → finish here”). Each case drawn from a different era — analog past → digital present → what’s next — so the walk itself tells the arc. Carries the AI-Jim quest below, and can layer in audio through the headphones handed out at the entrance.

02 · Exhibition / expo

A booth version

The same idea condensed for a trade show or exhibition floor — a single station that hooks passers-by and leaves them with the one thing we want them to remember.

03 · Daily employee flow

The everyday version

Staff pass fixed points inside the company every day. A light, rotating activation on those routes keeps the story — and the message of the season — in front of them.

The centerpiece: the AI-Jim quest

Talk to Jim. Earn the answer.

A conversational game where visitors talk to an AI “Jim” and have to work him — he only hands over the hint once they meet a condition, e.g. name the Glidewell product that changed the industry and five of its advantages.

The trick: visitors sell themselves. To get the answer they go read up on the product and its advantages — and end up reciting the pitch we never had to make. We ask a puzzle; they deliver the value proposition.

▶ Try the prototype

Prototype uses a rules-based grader; in production it runs on a live LLM that grades free-form answers.

The audio layer

Symposiums already hand out headphones and devices at the entrance. That’s a channel — pipe a short, well-produced audio track into the quest, so the experience isn’t only on a screen.

Where it lives: the people-flows map

Before designing more activations, map every point in the company where a flow of people meets a message we want to land — from symposiums down to the daily employee routes. For each: who flows through, and what they should take away. That map becomes the menu of places to drop an activation.
